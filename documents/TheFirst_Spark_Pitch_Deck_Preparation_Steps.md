# TheFirst Spark Challenge: Pitch Deck Preparation Steps

Use this sequence to build a pitch deck that makes it clear **who you are helping, what problem you are solving, and why your solution matters**.

## Step 1: State the challenge

Write the challenge your project is responding to.

> **Prompt:** We are responding to: `_______________________`

Keep this tied directly to the challenge brief rather than starting with your technology or product.

## Step 2: Define the first target user

Identify one specific initial user group.

> **Prompt:** Our first target user is: `_______________________`

Avoid broad labels such as “young women” or “healthcare workers.” Increase specificity using this progression:

1. **Industry or sector only:** “Healthcare workers”
2. **Role plus context:** “Nurses in public hospitals”
3. **Role plus moment:** “Nurses during patient discharge”
4. **Role plus moment plus struggle:** “Nurses managing high-volume discharge who struggle to confirm patients understood instructions”

Your target user should ideally reach Level 4: **role + moment + struggle**.

## Step 3: Identify the specific use case

Describe the exact moment or situation in which the problem occurs.

> **Prompt:** The moment where this problem happens is: `_______________________`

Do not describe only a general domain. Name the setting, trigger, or decision point where the user experiences the problem.

## Step 4: Describe the pain point

State what the user directly struggles with - the symptom they feel.

> **Prompt:** The user struggles with: `_______________________`

**Example:** Caregivers often leave the hospital confused about medication instructions, follow-up appointments, and warning signs to watch for.

## Step 5: Find the core bottleneck

Identify what prevents the user from solving the problem today - the root cause your solution must address.

> **Prompt:** This problem happens because: `_______________________`

**Example:** Information is given verbally or across multiple documents, and caregivers may not know what questions to ask before discharge.

Your solution must attack the bottleneck, not merely acknowledge the pain.

## Step 6: Explain the current alternative

Describe what users currently do instead of using your solution.

> **Prompt:** Today, they usually solve it by: `_______________________`

Include workarounds, manual processes, competing products, informal help, or the decision to do nothing.

## Step 7: Write the value proposition

Explain what useful outcome your solution creates for the target user.

> **Prompt:** Our solution helps them: `_______________________`

Focus on the improvement in the user's situation, not just the product's features.

## Step 8: Explain how the solution works

Show the product flow, workflow, or demo.

> **Prompt:** It works by: `_______________________`

Make the connection between the bottleneck and the mechanism of your solution explicit.

## Step 9: Combine the pitch spine into one sentence

Use this template:

> We help **[user]** who struggle with **[pain]** during **[use case]** by solving **[bottleneck]** through **[solution]** so they can **[valuable outcome]**.

A weak pitch names a technology and a broad sector:

> “We built an AI app that helps with healthcare.”

A stronger pitch identifies the user, situation, and outcome:

> “We help caregivers understand discharge instructions after a hospital visit, so they can manage medication, follow-ups, and warning signs more confidently.”

## Step 10: Build the deck in the recommended order

Structure the slides as follows:

1. **Challenge Statement** - What challenge are you responding to?
2. **Target User and Use Case** - Who exactly faces this problem, and when?
3. **Pain Point and Bottleneck** - What is hard, broken, slow, or confusing today?
4. **Current Alternative** - What do users currently do instead?
5. **Solution and Value Proposition** - What do you provide, and what outcome improves?
6. **How It Works** - Show the product flow, workflow, or demo.
7. **Traction and Validation (bonus)** - Add evidence, user feedback, or data that strengthens the pitch.

## Step 11: Add traction or validation where available

Include supporting evidence such as:

- User interviews or feedback
- Pilot results
- Usage or adoption data
- Prototype testing
- Letters of interest
- Relevant outcome measurements

This section is optional, but it can make the pitch more credible.

## Step 12: Run the judge-readiness check

Before presenting, confirm that a judge can answer all five questions:

- [ ] Who is this for?
- [ ] What real problem are they facing?
- [ ] What is the bottleneck?
- [ ] How does the solution remove that bottleneck?
- [ ] Why does this meaningfully address the challenge?

If all five answers are clear, the pitch is judge-ready.
